The Data Science Pipeline: Getting Started:

The first step is to acquire a dataset on which to perform the pipeline. When locating data to analyze, it is essential that the dataset selected meets three important criteria: The data is in a .csv file, which means that it is set up in the form of a comma separated list. This is important because it is this format that allows for performing operations to do analysis. The data set must be large. As any statistics class teaches, it is unreliable to make conclusions based on small amounts of data. The more information there is to work with, the more likely it is for trends found to be significant and accurate. The information that lies in the data holds promise to mean something, and to say something about the topic. While performing pipeline operations on any data requires a knowledge of that particular skill, if the analysis does not have purpose, there does not stand as much to be gained. One virtue of a good data scientist is the practiced ability to ask the right questions, and frame those questions such that the conclusions drawn are significant to the human experience.

The Data chosen here is Gun Deaths in the US: 2012-2014 found here via kaggle. This specific data set meets all criteria form above, being a csv file with 100,798 of entries; the data contains information about the year, month, intent (suicide, homicide, accidental, unidentified), sex, age, race, police involvement, education level, and location. Since this country suffers from the highest levels of civilian gun deaths in the world, it is useful to know and is humane to understand the intersectional ways in which these factors interact. The first step to making positive change is knowing the various breakdown of the problem.

This particular analysis of this data focuses largely on suicidal gun deaths, filtering out cases of police involvement, and then breaking those statistics down via total trends by age, then by age further broken down by sex, and then breaking that down even further by level of education. Then, finally, the data was broken down by race, and a regression was done to show how the average age is affected by this aspect.The reason for filtering and breaking data into smaller pieces is to see all the different aspects that make up the more simple statistics, and seeing what trends exist versus which factors do or do not have a different effect on these results.

Further breaking down the education data by race, and running a regression on that data, it is shown that while it is true across all races that average age does increase with an increased level of education, the difference is minimal in White, Black, and Hispanic communities. The difference is more pronounced, however amongst Asian/Pacific Islanders and Native American and Native Alsaskan people. The results of this regression mostly show that education level may have some affect, but overall does not cause any significant change to the average age of suicide by firearm; this finding upholds the conclusion made from analysis of the above violin plots.

This data also should be viewed and understood under the important cultural backdrop of the amount of governmental funding and resource allocation to the issue of gun deaths in the United States. A 1996 provision to the federal government omnibus spending bill, known as the Dickey Amendment, mandates that "none of the funds made available for injury prevention and control at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) may be used to advocate or promote gun control." As stated by the New York Times, this amendment “stripped $2.6 million from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention — the precise amount budgeted for a study of the health effects of shootings.” This legislation, put forth almost 25 years ago is still in effect today. This political landscape upon which strong research, analysis, and data depends, is critical to know and understand, and the reasons are twofold. 1. An organization like the CDC is heavily reliant on federal funding, and with no funding, the ability to put forth large, sweeping conclusions needed to help enact legal change are missing 2. Since the funding to research and analyze something is so pertinent to a highly prevalent modern health crisis, it is that much more important to analyze and pay attention to how different factors are at play within the data.
